One of the largest contributors to procrastination is **cognitive friction**. When a task feels too large, ambiguous, or complex, your brain struggles to find the entry point. The resulting anxiety is what makes you reach for your phone or seek out distractions.

To bypass this friction, you need to decompose your milestones.

## The Problem with "Big" Goals

Setting a goal like "Build checkout page" or "Write blog post" seems reasonable, but these are actually multi-step projects. If you start a 30-minute timer with that goal, your brain spends the first 10 minutes figuring out *where* to start, creating anxiety and reducing focus time.

A **checkpoint checklist** bridges the gap between the vision and the execution.

## The Rules of Decomposition

When breaking down a goal for a single focus session, follow these three rules:

1. **Keep it under 5 Checkpoints:** If your list has 15 items, your focus session is too broad. Aim for 3 to 5 clear, bite-sized tasks.
2. **Make them Actionable:** Start each checkpoint with a verb (e.g., "Draft layout", "Write database query", "Style primary button").
3. **Keep them under 15 minutes each:** Each checkpoint should represent a small milestone. Checking items off releases micro-doses of dopamine, building momentum to carry you through the rest of the session.

## Example: From Overwhelming to Executable

Let's look at how you can decompose a writing task:

*   **Vague Goal:** "Write newsletter."
*   **Decomposed Goal with Checkpoints:**
    *   [ ] Draft the main educational tip (200 words).
    *   [ ] Write the subject line variations.
    *   [ ] Add the links and calls-to-action.
    *   [ ] Run spelling and formatting checks.
    *   [ ] Send test email.

By laying out the exact sequence, you don't have to think about "what's next". You simply look at your checkpoints and check them off one by one.
